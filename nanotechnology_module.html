NANOTECHNOLOGY MODULE

INTENDED LEARNING OUTCOMES

  • Define nanoscience and nanotechnology along with related concepts.
  • Identify the nanoscale and understand the unique properties of nanomaterials.
  • Describe the historical development and key milestones of nanotechnology.
  • Explain the applications of nanotechnology across industries.
  • Discuss potential ethical, social, and safety concerns associated with nanotechnology.

DEFINING THE TERMS

Nanoscience. Nanoscience studies materials and phenomena at the nanoscale, typically between 1 and 100 nanometers (Levin, Jensen, & Kreimer, 2016). It combines physics, chemistry, biology, and engineering (Papadiamantis et al., 2020).

Nanotechnology. Nanotechnology deals with manipulating matter on atomic, molecular, and supramolecular scales (Cavaco-Paulo & Hofman, 2013). Key concepts include:

  • Nanoparticles. Tiny particles in the nanometer range used in medicine, electronics, and energy (Ventola, 2012).
  • Nanoelectronics. Development of electronic devices at the nanoscale (Cavaco-Paulo & Hofman, 2013).
  • Nanoscale. Refers to structures between 1 and 100 nanometers (Curran et al., 2010).
  • Nanomaterials. Materials with at least one dimension in the nanoscale, with unique properties compared to bulk materials (Ventola, 2012).

Examples of nanomaterials and applications:

  • Polymer dots (Pdots). Fluorescent particles for biosensing and cell imaging (Zhang et al., 2021).
  • Quantum dots (QDs). Semiconductor nanocrystals used in imaging, biosensing, and cancer therapy (Dias et al., 2021).
  • Silicon nanorods (SiNRs). Used as fluorescent sensors for biosensing (Zhang et al., 2021).
  • Gold nanoparticles (AuNPs). Optical and electronic applications in imaging and therapy (Dias et al., 2021).
  • Carbon nanomaterials. Include carbon nanotubes, graphene, and fullerenes used in therapy and photothermal applications (Kim et al., 2021).
  • Beads. Microscale particles for aptamer selection (Kim et al., 2021).
  • Other nanoparticles. Magnetic and metal oxide particles for therapy and molecular applications (Liu et al., 2020).

NANOTECHNOLOGY'S HUMBLE BEGINNINGS

  • 4th Century. Roman artisans added gold and silver nanoparticles to glass for color-changing effects (Lycurgus Cup).
  • Medieval Era. European stained-glass windows used nanoscale metals for vibrant colors.
  • 1959. Richard Feynman's speech “There is plenty of room at the bottom” sparked nanoscale manipulation interest.
  • 1974. Term "nanotechnology" coined by Norio Taniguchi.
  • 1981. Scanning tunneling microscope (STM) invented by Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer.
  • 1986. Nobel Prize awarded to Binnig and Rohrer for STM.
  • 1991. Discovery of carbon nanotubes by Sumio Iijima.
  • 1998. Don Eigler arranged 35 xenon atoms to spell "IBM," demonstrating precise atom manipulation.
  • 2000. National Nanotechnology Initiative (NNI) launched in the US.
  • 2016. Nobel Prize in Chemistry awarded for nanoscale molecular machines (Sauvage, Stoddart, Feringa).

APPLICATIONS OF NANOTECHNOLOGY

Nanotechnology enables innovative solutions across industries by controlling materials at the nanoscale. Key applications include:

  • Smart Textile Industry. Fabrics with water resistance, stain resistance, and UV protection using graphene oxide and TiO2 (Shah et al., 2022).
  • Cosmetics. Improved product efficacy using nanotechnology, e.g., liposomes since 1961 (Mittapally & Aziz, 2019).
  • Medicine. Drug delivery, imaging, sensors, and tissue engineering (Zheng et al., 2022; Abedini-Nassab et al., 2022).
  • Soil Remediation. Removal of contaminants using iron nanoparticles and carbon nanotubes (Kristanti et al., 2021).
  • Forest Management. Nanomaterials enhance water, energy, and production efficiency in forests (Singh et al., 2021).
  • Water and Wastewater Treatment. Nanomaterials improve filtration and removal of contaminants (Qu et al., 2013).
  • Plant Growth and Crop Protection. Nanoparticles optimize fertilizer and pesticide delivery; nanobiosensors monitor conditions (Shang et al., 2019).
  • Food Packaging and Safety. Silver nanoparticles as antimicrobials and nanosensors for contamination detection (Duncan, 2011).

ETHICAL ISSUES OF NANOTECHNOLOGY

  • Health Concerns. Nanoparticles can enter the body via inhalation, ingestion, or skin contact and may cause health risks (Akkam & Khatatbeh, 2021).
  • Human Enhancement. Ethical implications of improving human capabilities like memory, strength, or intelligence are unclear (Buruk et al., 2023).
  • Environmental Concerns. Nanoparticles may impact the environment long-term, including industrial waste (Anyansi-Archibong & Udoka, 2010).
  • Regulation. Lack of consistent regulations can lead to unethical practices; voluntary standards are recommended (Anand et al., 2011).
  • Social Concerns. Nanotechnology could widen inequalities between those who can afford access and those who cannot (Anyansi-Archibong & Udoka, 2010).

SUMMARY

Nanotechnology offers significant benefits across industries but requires careful consideration of ethical, health, environmental, and social implications. Responsible regulation, risk assessment, and ethical practices are essential for safe and equitable use.
